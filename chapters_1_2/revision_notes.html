Chapter P: Basic Concepts of Algebra

P.1 The Real Numbers and Their Properties

First Principles: Mathematics builds complex systems from simple axioms. The real number system \(\mathbb{R}\) is the foundation of continuous mathematics, constructed systematically from basic counting numbers.
Classifying Numbers & Sets

Natural Numbers (\(\mathbb{N}\)): \(\{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\) (Counting numbers).
Whole Numbers (\(\mathbb{W}\)): \(\{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\) (Includes zero).
Integers (\(\mathbb{Z}\)): \(\{\ldots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) (Includes negatives).
Rational Numbers (\(\mathbb{Q}\)): Numbers that can be expressed as \(\frac{p}{q}\) where \(p, q \in \mathbb{Z}\) and \(q \neq 0\). Their decimals terminate or repeat.
Irrational Numbers (\(\mathbb{I}\)): Cannot be expressed as a fraction of integers (e.g., \(\pi, \sqrt{2}\)). Decimals do not terminate or repeat.

Intervals, Absolute Value, and Distance

An interval represents a continuous segment of the real number line. We use brackets \([a, b]\) to include endpoints and parentheses \((a, b)\) to exclude them.

Absolute Value: \( |x| = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x \ge 0 \\ -x & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases} \)
Distance Formula (1D): \( d(a,b) = |b - a| \)
Order of Operations and Properties

We use PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction) to evaluate algebraic expressions. Key properties include Commutativity (\(a+b = b+a\)), Associativity (\((a+b)+c = a+(b+c)\)), and Distributivity (\(a(b+c) = ab+ac\)).

P.2 Integer Exponents and Scientific Notation

First Principles: Exponents are a shorthand for repeated multiplication, allowing us to describe incredibly large or small quantities compactly.
Product Rule: \( a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n} \)
Quotient Rule: \( \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \)
Power Rule: \( (a^m)^n = a^{mn} \)
Zero Exponent: \( a^0 = 1 \) (for \(a \neq 0\))
Negative Exponent: \( a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \)
Scientific Notation

A number written as \(c \times 10^n\), where \(1 \le c < 10\) and \(n\) is an integer. Used to express very large or very small values efficiently.

Example: Simplification
Simplify \( \left( \frac{2x^3y^{-2}}{4x^{-1}y^4} \right)^{-2} \)
First, simplify the inside: \( \frac{2}{4} x^{3 - (-1)} y^{-2 - 4} = \frac{1}{2} x^4 y^{-6} = \frac{x^4}{2y^6} \)
Apply the negative exponent: \( \left( \frac{x^4}{2y^6} \right)^{-2} = \left( \frac{2y^6}{x^4} \right)^2 = \frac{4y^{12}}{x^8} \)

P.3 Polynomials

First Principles: Polynomials are algebraic expressions built strictly from addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents. They behave similarly to integers under arithmetic operations.

Polynomial Vocabulary: A polynomial of degree \(n\) has the form \(a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_1x + a_0\). The highest exponent is the degree, and \(a_n\) is the leading coefficient.

Special Products

Recognizing special product patterns speeds up algebraic manipulation significantly.

Difference of Squares: \( (a - b)(a + b) = a^2 - b^2 \)
Perfect Square Trinomial: \( (a \pm b)^2 = a^2 \pm 2ab + b^2 \)
Sum of Cubes: \( (a+b)(a^2-ab+b^2) = a^3+b^3 \)
Difference of Cubes: \( (a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2) = a^3-b^3 \)

P.4 Factoring Polynomials

First Principles: Factoring is the process of un-multiplying. It breaks down complex expressions into their fundamental, irreducible parts, which is essential for solving equations and finding roots.
Factoring Techniques
  • Greatest Common Monomial Factor (GCF): Always check for a common factor first (e.g., \(4x^3 - 6x^2 = 2x^2(2x - 3)\)).
  • Grouping: For polynomials with 4 terms, group into pairs and factor GCFs (e.g., \(x^3 - 3x^2 + 2x - 6 = x^2(x-3) + 2(x-3) = (x^2+2)(x-3)\)).
  • Trinomials (\(ax^2+bx+c\)): If \(a=1\), find two numbers that multiply to \(c\) and add to \(b\). If \(a \neq 1\), use the AC Method (split the middle term).
Example: AC Method for \(Ax^2+Bx+C\)
Factor \( 6x^2 + 11x - 10 \)
Multiply \(A \times C = 6 \times (-10) = -60\).
Find factors of \(-60\) that add to \(11\): \(15\) and \(-4\).
Rewrite the middle term: \( 6x^2 + 15x - 4x - 10 \)
Factor by grouping: \( 3x(2x + 5) - 2(2x + 5) = (3x - 2)(2x + 5) \)

P.5 Rational Expressions

First Principles: A rational expression is a ratio of two polynomials. It behaves exactly like numerical fractions, subject to the rule that the denominator cannot be zero (Domain Restrictions).
Operations on Rational Expressions

Simplifying: Factor the numerator and denominator completely, then cancel common factors.
Multiplying/Dividing: Multiply across. For division, multiply by the reciprocal of the second expression.
Adding/Subtracting: You MUST find the Least Common Denominator (LCD). Factor all denominators to build the LCD efficiently.

Complex Fractions

A fraction containing fractions in the numerator or denominator. Simplify by multiplying the entire numerator and denominator by the LCD of all minor fractions.

P.6 Rational Exponents and Radicals

First Principles: Radicals and rational exponents are two languages describing the same concept. \(x^{m/n}\) implies a fractional power where the numerator is the exponent and the denominator is the root index.
Rational Exponent: \( a^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m \)
Product/Quotient Rules: \( \sqrt[n]{ab} = \sqrt[n]{a}\sqrt[n]{b} \) and \( \sqrt[n]{\frac{a}{b}} = \frac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}} \)
Rationalizing Expressions

To rationalize a denominator containing a binomial with radicals (e.g., \(a + \sqrt{b}\)), multiply the numerator and denominator by its conjugate (\(a - \sqrt{b}\)). This exploits the Difference of Squares to eliminate the radical: \((a+\sqrt{b})(a-\sqrt{b}) = a^2 - b\).

Chapter 1: Equations and Inequalities

1.1 Linear Equations in One Variable

Definitions: An equation states two algebraic expressions are equal. A linear equation can be written as \(ax + b = 0\).
Types of Equations:

  • Identity: True for all real numbers (e.g., \(2(x+1) = 2x+2\)). Solution set is \(\mathbb{R}\).
  • Conditional: True for specific values (e.g., \(3x = 12\)).
  • Inconsistent: True for no values (e.g., \(x = x + 1\)). Solution set is empty \(\emptyset\).

1.2 Applications of Linear Equations: Modeling

First Principles: Modeling translates physical phenomena into mathematical equations, allowing us to solve real-world problems using algebra.

Finance (Interest): \( I = Prt \) (Interest = Principal \(\times\) Rate \(\times\) Time).
Uniform Motion: \( d = rt \) (Distance = Rate \(\times\) Time). Often involves equating times or summing distances.
Work Rate: If person A does a job in \(a\) hours, their rate is \(1/a\). Combined rate: \(\frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} = \frac{1}{t_{\text{total}}}\).
Mixtures: Use the equation \( (\text{Amount 1})(\text{Concentration 1}) + (\text{Amount 2})(\text{Concentration 2}) = (\text{Total Amount})(\text{Final Concentration}) \).

1.3 Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation is of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) \((a \neq 0)\).

Solving Methods
  • Factoring: Relies on the Zero-Product Property. If \(AB = 0\), then \(A=0\) or \(B=0\).
  • Square Root Method: If \(x^2 = k\), then \(x = \pm \sqrt{k}\).
  • Completing the Square: To make \(x^2 + bx\) a perfect square, add \((b/2)^2\).
  • Quadratic Formula: \( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)
The Discriminant (\(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac\)):
\(\Delta > 0\): Two distinct real solutions.
\(\Delta = 0\): One repeated real solution.
\(\Delta < 0\): Two complex conjugate solutions.

Golden Rectangle: A rectangle where the ratio of length to width is the Golden Ratio \(\phi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618\).

1.4 Complex Numbers

First Principles: The imaginary unit \(i\) is defined as \(\sqrt{-1}\), meaning \(i^2 = -1\). A complex number has the form \(a + bi\) (Standard Form), where \(a\) is the real part and \(b\) is the imaginary part.
Operations

Addition/Subtraction: Add/subtract real and imaginary parts independently.
Multiplication: Use FOIL and replace \(i^2\) with \(-1\).
Division: Multiply numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator (\(a - bi\)) to yield a real number in the denominator: \((a+bi)(a-bi) = a^2 + b^2\).

1.5 Solving Other Types of Equations

Rational Equations: Multiply all terms by the LCD to clear fractions. Warning: You must check for extraneous solutions that make the original denominator zero.
Equations with Radicals: Isolate the radical, then raise both sides to the appropriate power. Warning: Squaring both sides often introduces extraneous solutions. Always check your answers!
Quadratic in Form: Equations that can be written as \(au^2 + bu + c = 0\) through a substitution (e.g., \(x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = 0\) let \(u = x^2\)).

1.6 Inequalities

Solving linear inequalities is identical to solving linear equations, with one massive exception:
Whenever you multiply or divide an inequality by a negative number, you MUST reverse the direction of the inequality sign.

Polynomial and Rational Inequalities (Test Points)

For non-linear inequalities (e.g., \(x^2 - x - 6 > 0\)):
1. Move everything to one side so the other is 0.
2. Find the roots (or undefined points) to establish boundary points on a number line.
3. Pick a test point in each interval and plug it into the inequality to see if it yields a positive or negative result.

1.7 Equations and Inequalities Involving Absolute Value

Absolute value \(|X|\) is the distance of \(X\) from the origin.

Equation: \( |X| = k \) means \( X = k \) or \( X = -k \).
"Less Than" Inequality (\(|X| < k\)): Represents a bounded interval between \(-k\) and \(k\). Setup: \( -k < X < k \).
"Greater Than" Inequality (\(|X| > k\)): Represents the outer wings extending to infinity. Setup: \( X < -k \) OR \( X > k \).

Chapter 2: Graphs and Functions

2.1 The Coordinate Plane

First Principles: The Cartesian coordinate system links algebra and geometry. Any algebraic equation with two variables forms a geometric shape when its solutions are plotted as points \((x,y)\).
Distance Formula: \( d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \) (Derived from Pythagorean Theorem)
Midpoint Formula: \( M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right) \) (Averages of the coordinates)

2.2 Graphs of Equations

Intercepts: To find the x-intercept, let \(y=0\). To find the y-intercept, let \(x=0\).

Symmetry
  • x-axis symmetry: Replacing \(y\) with \(-y\) yields an equivalent equation.
  • y-axis symmetry: Replacing \(x\) with \(-x\) yields an equivalent equation.
  • Origin symmetry: Replacing both \(x\) with \(-x\) and \(y\) with \(-y\) yields an equivalent equation.
Equation of a Circle: \( (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2 \)
Center = \((h,k)\), Radius = \(r\)

Semicircles: Solving a circle equation for \(y\) gives \(y = k \pm \sqrt{r^2 - (x-h)^2}\). The positive root represents the top semicircle; the negative root is the bottom.

2.3 Lines

Slope (\(m\)) represents the average rate of change: \( m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \).

Forms of a Linear Equation
  • Point-Slope Form: \( y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \) (Best for writing equations).
  • Slope-Intercept Form: \( y = mx + b \) (Best for graphing).
  • General Form: \( Ax + By = C \).

Parallel Lines: Slopes are exactly the same (\(m_1 = m_2\)).
Perpendicular Lines: Slopes are negative reciprocals (\(m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1\) or \(m_2 = -1/m_1\)).
Linear Regression: Statistical process of finding the line of best fit for scattered data.

2.4 Functions

First Principles: A function \(f\) is a rule that assigns to each input element \(x\) in the Domain exactly ONE output element \(y\) in the Range.

Vertical Line Test: If any vertical line intersects a graph in more than one point, the graph does NOT represent a function.
Domain Rules: Denominators cannot equal zero. Expressions inside even-indexed roots (like square roots) must be \(\ge 0\).

2.5 Properties of Functions

Increasing/Decreasing: A function is increasing if, as \(x\) moves right, \(y\) goes up. It is decreasing if \(y\) goes down.
Relative Extrema: The highest/lowest points in a local region of the graph (peaks and valleys).
Average Rate of Change: The slope of the secant line connecting two points on the function: \( \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \).

Even and Odd Functions

Even Function: \(f(-x) = f(x)\). Symmetric about the y-axis (e.g., \(y=x^2, y=|x|\)).
Odd Function: \(f(-x) = -f(x)\). Symmetric about the origin (e.g., \(y=x^3, y=1/x\)).

2.6 A Library of Functions

You must know the shape, domain, and range of these parent graphs intimately:
- Linear (\(f(x) = x\))
- Square (\(f(x) = x^2\))
- Cube (\(f(x) = x^3\))
- Square Root (\(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\))
- Cube Root (\(f(x) = \sqrt[3]{x}\))
- Absolute Value (\(f(x) = |x|\))
- Reciprocal (\(f(x) = 1/x\))

Piecewise Functions: Functions defined by different rules over different parts of their domain. Always pay attention to the inequality domain conditions when evaluating or graphing.

2.7 Transformations of Functions

Given the graph of \(y = f(x)\) and positive constants \(c, k\):

  • Vertical Shifts: \( f(x) \pm k \) (Up/Down)
  • Horizontal Shifts: \( f(x \pm c) \) (Left/Right) — Opposite of what you'd expect! (\(x-c\) shifts right).
  • Reflections: \(-f(x)\) reflects across the x-axis. \(f(-x)\) reflects across the y-axis.
  • Vertical Stretch/Compress: \(a f(x)\). Stretches vertically if \(a > 1\). Compresses if \(0 < a < 1\).
  • Horizontal Stretch/Compress: \(f(bx)\). Compresses horizontally if \(b > 1\). Stretches if \(0 < b < 1\).

2.8 Combining Functions; Composite Functions

Operations: \((f \pm g)(x) = f(x) \pm g(x)\). The domain is the intersection of the domains of \(f\) and \(g\). For \((f/g)(x)\), additionally exclude any \(x\) where \(g(x) = 0\).

Composition of Functions

\( (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) \). The output of the inner function \(g(x)\) becomes the input for the outer function \(f\).
Domain of Composition: Must satisfy the domain of the inner function \(g(x)\) AND the domain of the final composed function \(f(g(x))\).

2.9 Inverse Functions

First Principles: An inverse function \(f^{-1}\) perfectly undoes the action of \(f\). If \(f\) maps \(x \to y\), then \(f^{-1}\) maps \(y \to x\). Graphically, it is a reflection across the line \(y = x\).

One-to-One Requirement: A function only has an inverse if it passes the Horizontal Line Test (every output has exactly one input).
Property of Inverses: \( f(f^{-1}(x)) = x \) AND \( f^{-1}(f(x)) = x \).
Domain & Range: The domain of \(f\) becomes the range of \(f^{-1}\), and the range of \(f\) becomes the domain of \(f^{-1}\).

Example: Finding the Inverse Algebraically
Find \(f^{-1}(x)\) for \(f(x) = \frac{2x + 1}{x - 3}\).
1. Write as \(y = \frac{2x + 1}{x - 3}\).
2. Swap \(x\) and \(y\): \(x = \frac{2y + 1}{y - 3}\).
3. Solve for \(y\):
    \(x(y - 3) = 2y + 1\)
    \(xy - 3x = 2y + 1\)
    \(xy - 2y = 3x + 1\)
    \(y(x - 2) = 3x + 1\)
    \(y = \frac{3x + 1}{x - 2}\). Thus, \(f^{-1}(x) = \frac{3x + 1}{x - 2}\).
